# Tone, Humor & Marketing Identity

> This game doesn't take itself too seriously. The science is real. The magic is real. The comedy is mandatory.

**Status**: Exploration

---

## The Vibe

**Epic Rap Battles of History meets science class meets Saturday morning cartoons.** The game is grounded in real science and real history, but the presentation is irreverent, slapstick, and meme-literate. Famous scientists — otherwise depicted as stern portraits in textbooks — are reimagined as dramatic, petty, hilarious wizard rivals who bicker across centuries.

The tone is **Drunk History, not PBS Documentary.** The science should be accurate enough that players actually learn something, but the presentation should make them laugh first and think second.

### What We're NOT
- A trivia game that makes you feel dumb
- A serious "educational" product with jokes bolted on
- Ironic or cynical — the humor comes from love, not mockery
- So inside-baseball that non-science people feel excluded

### What We ARE
- A game where Archimedes in wizard robes streaks through Syracuse yelling "EUREKA!"
- A game where Tesla and Edison trash talk each other across the table
- A game where someone yells "I REFUTE!" and slams a card down
- A game where the card art makes people pull out their phones to take pictures

### Phrase Design Principles

1. **Short and yell-able** — if you can't shout it across the table, it's too long
2. **Academic but absurd** — the comedy is in using serious scientific language for a card game
3. **Quotable outside the game** — "I REFUTE!" should become something friends say to each other
4. **Layered** — casual players say "Eureka!", tryhards say "Your methodology is FLAWED!"
5. **Some are mandatory** — certain phrases are required by the rules and enforceable by other players (see Call-Out Mechanic below)

## The Call-Out Mechanic — "Say It or Pay For It"

The UNO model: certain game events **require** you to say a specific phrase. If you forget, any opponent can call you out and you suffer a penalty. This is the single most important mechanic for making the game memorable, social, and meme-worthy.

### Why This Works

UNO is one of the best-selling games in history, and "UNO!" is one of the most recognizable moments in all of gaming. The call-out mechanic is why:

1. **Creates tension** — every qualifying moment, you're watching opponents to see if they forget
2. **Creates stories** — "I can't believe you forgot to say Eureka and lost that combo bonus!"
3. **Levels the playing field** — experienced players can still forget under pressure; new players catch them
4. **Generates table talk** — players are watching, calling out, laughing, groaning
5. **Creates the game's identity** — people remember the PHRASES, not the point values
6. **Social media fuel** — clips of someone forgetting to say "Eureka!" or dramatically yelling "I REFUTE!" are shareable moments

### The Two Core Call-Outs

#### "EUREKA!" — Discovery Call-Out

**When**: You successfully play a **type combination** for the first time in the game (e.g., combining Fire + Earth to produce a Metal spell, or any multi-type combo). This is the game's core discovery moment — you've found something new.

**What you must say**: "Eureka!" (or any enthusiastic variant — "EUREKA!", "eureka...", etc.)

**If you forget**: Any opponent can call out **"Back to the lab!"** before your next action. Penalty: you lose the discovery bonus (bonus points, extra draw, or whatever the Eureka reward is). The combo still resolves, but you don't get the bonus for "discovering" it. You just stumbled onto it without realizing what you found — which is thematically perfect. The discovery only counts if you RECOGNIZE it.

**The reward for remembering**: A Eureka bonus — could be:
- Draw an extra card (you learned something from the discovery)
- Gain 1 Knowledge token (insight from the eureka moment)
- A small VP token (discovery points that accumulate)
- The first player to Eureka a specific combo gets a permanent "First Discovery" marker worth end-game VP

**Why it works thematically**: The whole game is about discovery as magic. The eureka moment IS the fantasy. Making players literally exclaim "Eureka!" when they discover something is the game's thesis statement made mechanical. Archimedes would be proud.

#### "COLLAB!" — Two Meanings, One Word

"Collab" serves double duty — it's both a solo call-out for cross-type synergies AND a request to work with another player. Both meanings are thematically perfect: science advances through collaboration, whether that's combining disciplines within your own lab or literally partnering with another researcher.

##### Meaning 1: Cross-Type Synergy (Solo Call-Out)

**When**: You combine discoveries, resources, or effects **across two different type families** in a single play. This is broader than Eureka — it's about cross-pollination. Examples:
- Using a Fire instrument's production to pay for a Water spell
- Chaining two different wizards' abilities together
- Playing a card that benefits from two different type affinities you've built
- Any moment where your engine creates value by connecting different branches of knowledge

**What you must say**: "Collab!"

**If you forget**: Any opponent can call out **"Solo project!"** Penalty: you lose the collaboration bonus. Your play still resolves, but you don't get the synergy reward. You did the work alone — no cross-disciplinary insight.

**The reward for remembering**: A Collab bonus — could be:
- The combo effect is amplified (e.g., +1 to damage, +1 to resource gain)
- You may immediately take a free minor action (the collaboration sparked a follow-up idea)
- Place a Collab token on one of the cards involved — cards with Collab tokens are worth bonus VP at end-game (representing landmark cross-disciplinary discoveries)

##### Meaning 2: Player-to-Player Collaboration (Request)

**When**: On your turn, you can declare **"Collab!"** directed at another player to propose a joint action. This is a negotiation/trading mechanic wrapped in the same word.

**How it works**:
1. Active player says "Collab!" and names a target player
2. The target can accept ("Let's publish!") or decline ("I work alone.")
3. If accepted, both players contribute something and both benefit — a joint discovery that neither could achieve solo

**The specialty principle**: Collabs are most powerful when each player brings a **different specialty** — a type, resource, or ability the other player doesn't have. This mirrors real science: the most impactful papers come from cross-disciplinary collaboration, not two people who study the same thing.

**Possible collab actions**:
- **Joint Discovery**: Both players reveal a type card from hand. If the two types form a valid combination (e.g., your Fire + their Earth = Metal), both players gain the resulting secondary/tertiary resource or effect. Neither player could have done this alone because they didn't have both types. The more distant the type families, the rarer and more valuable the joint discovery — a Fire specialist and a Water specialist producing something neither could reach alone is exactly the kind of cross-pollination that drives real breakthroughs.
- **Shared Research**: You let the other player look at the top X cards of the deck; they keep one, you keep one. Mutual benefit from shared effort.
- **Co-authorship**: Both players contribute resources toward a spell that's too expensive for either alone. Both get partial credit (VP split, or both get a smaller version of the effect). A Fire school wizard and an Earth school wizard co-authoring a Metal spell is the dream — neither has enough of the other's type to cast it solo.
- **Peer Exchange**: Trade cards, resources, or knowledge tokens directly. Simple, but framing it as "Collab!" instead of "trade" changes the tone entirely.
- **Joint Publication**: Both players spend resources to co-publish a high-tier spell or theory card from the shared display. The card goes into a shared "joint publications" area — it benefits both players (e.g., both get VP, both can use its effect) but neither fully owns it. Represents a co-authored paper.

**Specialty-driven incentives**:
- **Complementary types = stronger result**: If the two players contribute types from different families (e.g., primary + secondary, or two different primaries), the collab reward is amplified. Same-family contributions are weaker — you're just doing the same work twice.
- **School bonuses**: Certain wizard school pairings have historical affinity. A collab between the School of Current (Faraday, Electric) and the School of Fields (Maxwell, Magnetic) should produce an extra-powerful result — electromagnetism was literally born from this collaboration.
- **Unique-to-collab results**: Some secondary/tertiary types or spells can ONLY be produced through player collaboration, not solo play. This makes collabs strategically necessary, not just nice-to-have. If Metal requires Fire + Earth and you're deep in Fire but have no Earth, you NEED to find an Earth player. This creates a natural trading economy driven by specialization.
- **The specialist advantage**: The element exclusivity reward (fewer players on an element = stronger bonus, from balance-philosophy.md) creates natural specialists. If you're the only Fire player, your Fire resources are amplified — making you the most valuable collab partner for anyone who needs Fire. This turns the balance mechanic into a social mechanic: the lone specialist is in high demand.

##### Meaning 3: Using Another Player's Lab (Everdell-Style Open Access)

The third flavor of Collab draws from Everdell's Destination card mechanic — where another player's city contains worker spots you can visit. In our game: another player's lab contains instruments, wizards, and infrastructure that you can **tap into**.

**How it works**:
1. You say "Collab!" and point to a specific card in another player's lab (an instrument, a wizard, a theory)
2. You place an action/apprentice on their card to use its ability — as if visiting their lab
3. **You** get the benefit of the ability (resource generation, type conversion, draw, etc.)
4. **They** get a small reward for hosting you — 1 Knowledge token, 1 VP, or a minor resource (the "consulting fee")

**Why specialties drive this**: If you're a Fire specialist and another player has a Crucible (produces Metal from Fire + Earth), you can visit their lab to use their Crucible with your abundant Fire resources. You get Metal. They get a consulting fee. Neither of you could have gotten as much value alone.

**The open lab economy**: Certain cards in your lab could be designated as **"Open Research"** (like Everdell's red Destination cards) — they have a worker spot symbol, and any player can place there. Other cards are **"Classified"** — only you can use them. This creates a choice when building your lab:
- Open Research cards attract visitors (you get consulting fees, which is passive income)
- Classified cards keep your advantages private (no one can piggyback on your engine)
- Trade-off dilemma: do you build a lab that earns passive income from visitors, or a closed lab that hoards its output?

**Thematic fit**: This is how real academic labs work. Visiting researchers use another lab's equipment all the time. The host lab gets co-authorship credit. Sometimes the visitor makes the breakthrough, sometimes the host does. It's messy, competitive, and collaborative all at once.

**Interaction with the call-out**: When you use another player's lab, you say "Collab!" — same word as the cross-type synergy and the partnership request. This means "Collab!" is heard constantly at the table in different contexts, hammering the word into the game's identity.

**Why this creates interesting decisions**:
- **Who do you collab with?** The leading player is the most dangerous partner — you're helping them too. The trailing player is safer but may have less to offer. The player with complementary types is the most valuable.
- **When do you collab?** Early collabs build relationships and unlock combos you can't reach alone. Late collabs risk giving your opponent the last piece they need to win.
- **Accept or decline?** Declining is safe but isolating. Accepting helps you but also helps them. This is a classic trade-off dilemma.
- **Betrayal potential**: If a Collab reveals information (your hand, your strategy), the other player now knows more about you. The "collaboration" gave them intelligence. Very historically accurate — Priestley told Lavoisier about oxygen over dinner, and Lavoisier used it to destroy Priestley's theory.

**Thematic resonance**: Real science is collaborative. Papers have co-authors. Labs share results. But collaborators also compete for credit, priority, and Nobel Prizes. The dual nature of "Collab" — cooperative and competitive simultaneously — mirrors the reality of scientific collaboration perfectly.

**Interaction with rivalries**: If two players' wizard schools have a historical rivalry (Tesla vs. Edison, Newton vs. Hooke), a Collab between them could be extra dramatic — "unlikely allies" — or could have bonus/penalty effects. Rival schools collaborating might produce a stronger result (the tension creates breakthroughs) or might have a higher chance of "betrayal" (one player gets more than the other).

##### Why One Word Works for Both

The ambiguity is a feature. When someone says "Collab!" the table has a moment of "wait, are they calling out a cross-type synergy or asking someone to partner?" The context makes it clear, but the shared vocabulary means the word gets said MORE often, reinforcing it as part of the game's identity. It's the same word for the same concept at different scales — combining different things to create something new.

**Why it works thematically**: The greatest breakthroughs in science came from collaboration — whether that's one mind combining chemistry and biology, or two researchers pooling their expertise. The word means the same thing at every scale: cross-pollination creates discovery. This maps directly to the "World of Interaction" design principle — everything connects to everything, including the players.

### Additional Call-Out Candidates

Beyond the two core call-outs, these could be optional or variant-mode additions:

| Trigger | Required Phrase | Forget Penalty Call-Out | Penalty | Notes |
|---------|----------------|------------------------|---------|-------|
| Playing your **last card** | **"Final thesis!"** | "Incomplete bibliography!" | Draw 2 cards (you're not done yet) | Direct UNO parallel — most intuitive for players |
| **Countering** an opponent's play | **"I refute!"** | No call-out needed — the counter fails if you don't declare it | Counter doesn't activate | Makes countering dramatic and deliberate |
| Completing all stages of your **school board** | **"I declare this a LAW!"** | "Needs more peer review!" | Lose 1 stage bonus VP | Big dramatic moment for the player who finishes |
| Achieving a **breakthrough** (milestone) | **"Published!"** | "Scooped!" (someone else can claim it) | Next player in turn order can steal the breakthrough | Racing mechanic with social enforcement |
| **Winning the game** | **"Nobel Prize!"** | — | No penalty, just satisfaction | Signature victory call |

### Call-Out Timing Rules

Clear timing prevents arguments:

1. **Window**: An opponent can call you out **any time before your next action or before the next player begins their turn** — whichever comes first. Once the moment passes, the window closes.
2. **Who can call out**: Any opponent, not just the next player. First to call it out gets... nothing? Or maybe: the caller draws a card / gains 1 Knowledge as a "peer review" reward for being attentive. This incentivizes watching the table.
3. **Disputes**: If there's a disagreement about whether a Eureka/Collab moment occurred (was that actually a new combo?), the player to the left of the active player is the "referee." Quick, clean, no debate.
4. **Stacking**: If a single play triggers BOTH Eureka and Collab (a new combo that also crosses type families), you need to say both. "Eureka! Collab!" Forgetting either one can be called out independently. These double moments should be rare and exciting.

### The Social Loop

Here's why this mechanic creates the game's identity:

```
Player makes a combo
        ↓
Do they remember to say "EUREKA!"?
   ↓                    ↓
  YES                   NO
   ↓                    ↓
Table cheers,        Opponent yells
player gets bonus    "BACK TO THE LAB!"
   ↓                    ↓
Memorable moment     Memorable moment
(triumph)            (comedy)
   ↓                    ↓
   └──→ Either way, a story gets told ←──┘
```

Both outcomes are fun. Remembering feels triumphant. Forgetting is hilarious. Calling someone out feels clever. Getting called out is a funny "oh no" moment, not a devastating loss. The penalties should sting enough to matter but not enough to ruin the game.

### Comparison to UNO

| Aspect | UNO | This Game |
|--------|-----|-----------|
| **Required phrase** | "UNO!" (one card left) | "Eureka!" (new discovery) / "Collab!" (cross-type combo) |
| **Call-out** | Any opponent says "UNO!" before you | "Back to the lab!" / "Solo project!" |
| **Penalty** | Draw 2 cards | Lose discovery/collab bonus |
| **Frequency** | Once per hand (endgame only) | Multiple times per game (throughout) |
| **Feel** | Defensive (protect your lead) | Celebratory (announce your achievement) |
| **Social dynamic** | Catching someone who's about to win | Catching someone who's too focused on strategy to celebrate |

Key difference: UNO's call-out is defensive (you're protecting a lead). Ours is **celebratory** — you're announcing a discovery. This means the phrase is associated with positive, exciting moments rather than anxiety. Players WANT to say "Eureka!" — it's fun. The call-out catches people who are too deep in their head to enjoy the moment, which is itself a funny and thematically resonant idea: the absentminded scientist who makes a breakthrough but doesn't notice.

### Frequency Tuning

This mechanic only works if the moments happen at the right frequency:
- **Too rare** (once per game): players forget the rule exists. No habit forms.
- **Too frequent** (every turn): becomes annoying, rote. Players tune it out.
- **Sweet spot** (2-4 times per player per game): frequent enough to stay top-of-mind, rare enough that each one feels like a real moment.

The definition of what counts as a "Eureka" moment vs. a "Collab" moment needs to be tuned to hit this frequency. Rough targets:
- **Eureka**: First time you play a specific type combination in this game. With 16 types and ~120 unique pairs, even a fraction of those appearing in a single game gives plenty of Eureka moments without overwhelming.
- **Collab**: When a single play involves 2+ different type families. This should be slightly rarer than Eureka — a "Collab" is a bigger deal, a cross-disciplinary moment.

## Scientist Rivalries — Wizard Battles

### Historical Rivalries (Documented Feuds)

These are real conflicts between scientists who are already in the game as wizard school heads/pupils. These can drive card art, flavor text, rivalry mechanics, and marketing.

#### Tesla vs. Edison — The War of Currents
**Schools**: Magnetic (Tesla, pupil) vs. ??? (Edison not currently in game)
**The Feud**: Tesla worked for Edison, who promised him $50,000 and then said "you don't understand our American humor" when Tesla delivered. Tesla quit. Edison then ran a propaganda campaign electrocuting animals with AC to prove Tesla's current was dangerous. He literally developed the electric chair to make people associate AC with death.
**The Payoff**: AC won. Tesla died alone with his pigeons. Edison died rich and famous.
**Card Concept**: Rivalry card — "War of Currents." Both players choose AC or DC simultaneously. If different, the AC player wins. If same, the DC player wins. (Prediction dilemma!)
**Visual**: Tesla and Edison facing off, lightning between them, but Edison's side has a suspiciously placed elephant behind him.

#### Newton vs. Hooke — The Portrait Destroyer
**Schools**: Earth (Newton, head) vs. ??? (Hooke not in game)
**The Feud**: Newton's "standing on the shoulders of giants" was probably an insult about Hooke's short stature. Newton systematically erased Hooke from the Principia. After Hooke died, the ONLY portrait of him mysteriously disappeared while Newton was president of the Royal Society.
**Card Concept**: "Erased from History" — remove target card from an opponent's lab. It's gone. No record.
**Visual**: Newton with a conspicuously empty frame on the wall behind him, holding a suspiciously portrait-sized bonfire.

#### Newton vs. Leibniz — The Calculus War
**Schools**: Earth (Newton, head) vs. ??? (Leibniz not in game)
**The Feud**: Both invented calculus independently. Newton, as president of the Royal Society, appointed a committee to investigate priority — then secretly wrote the committee's report himself. He also anonymously reviewed his own report, praising it. Peak academic pettiness.
**Card Concept**: "Priority Dispute" — claim an opponent's discovery as your own? Some kind of credit-stealing mechanic.
**Visual**: Newton at a desk, writing a report, wearing a fake mustache disguise. The report title reads "IMPARTIAL INVESTIGATION (by Definitely Not Newton)."

#### Lavoisier vs. Priestley — The Dinner Party Betrayal
**Schools**: Fire (Lavoisier, head) vs. ??? (Priestley not in game)
**The Feud**: Priestley isolated oxygen and told Lavoisier about it over dinner. Lavoisier then used it to overthrow the entire theoretical framework Priestley believed in. Priestley went to his grave defending phlogiston — a theory his own discovery disproved.
**Card Concept**: "Research Dinner" — gain information from an opponent's hand, then use it against them next turn.
**Visual**: Two wizards at a dinner table. One is excitedly explaining something over wine. The other is taking notes with a sinister smile.

#### Bohr vs. Einstein — The Breakfast Debates
**Schools**: Radioactive area (Bohr) vs. Cosmic (Einstein not directly in game but mapped nearby)
**The Feud**: At the Solvay Conferences, Einstein came to breakfast every morning with a new thought experiment to disprove quantum mechanics. Bohr spent all day refuting it. Next morning, Einstein had another one. When Bohr died in 1962, the photon box diagram was on his blackboard — still thinking about it.
**Card Concept**: "Thought Experiment" — pose a challenge. If opponent can't answer it this turn, gain advantage. (Prediction + trade-off dilemma)
**Visual**: Two wizards at a breakfast table. One has a chalkboard with equations. The other is face-down in his cereal, exhausted. A speech bubble says "What if..."

#### Cope vs. Marsh — The Bone Wars
**Schools**: Both Earth-adjacent
**The Feud**: Rival paleontologists who bribed each other's workers, dynamited fossil sites, and published scathing personal attacks. Cope put a dinosaur head on the wrong end and Marsh never let him forget it.
**Card Concept**: "Sabotage" — destroy an opponent's resource-generating card. "Fossil Fraud" — play a card face-down that turns out to be incorrectly assembled (bluffing mechanic).
**Visual**: Two wizards in a canyon, wands pointed at each other's dig sites, fossils flying, dynamite everywhere. One dinosaur skeleton has its head on its tail.

#### Rosalind Franklin vs. Watson & Crick — Photo 51
**Schools**: Crystal (could map to Crystal school)
**The Feud**: Franklin's X-ray crystallography image (Photo 51) was shown to Watson and Crick without her consent. They used it to discover DNA's structure. She died before the Nobel. Watson then wrote a book describing her dismissively.
**Card Concept**: "Stolen Research" — use an opponent's revealed information to accelerate your own discovery.
**Visual**: A wizard peering through a crystal at a glowing double helix image, while behind her another wizard is photographing her notes.

#### Semmelweis vs. Everyone — The Reflex
**Schools**: Water/Poison adjacent
**The Feud**: Discovered handwashing prevents death. Was committed to an asylum for it. Died of the infection he spent his life trying to prevent. The "Semmelweis Reflex" — rejecting evidence because it's inconvenient — is named after him.
**Card Concept**: "Semmelweis Reflex" — opponent can choose to ignore your evidence... but pays for it later.

### Rivalry Mechanics

Rivalries could be a game mechanic:

1. **Rivalry Cards**: Special cards depicting two scientists. When both are in play (even across different players), they trigger bonus effects or conflicts.
2. **Debate/Refutation**: When a rivalry pair faces off, a special resolution occurs — possibly the prediction dilemma (simultaneous reveal).
3. **Historical Grudge**: Certain wizard cards get bonuses when played against their historical rival's school.

## The Debate Mechanic (Exploratory)

> What if players could mock-debate for priority?

### The Concept

When two players both want the same resource, card, or breakthrough — instead of a die roll or priority rule — they **debate**. Each player makes a brief (10-second) argument for why they deserve it. Other players vote. Tie goes to... something.

### How It Could Work

1. **Trigger**: Two players attempt the same action simultaneously, or a "Dispute" card is played
2. **The Debate**: Each player gets one sentence (timed). They can argue in-character as their wizard school, cite game state, or just be funny.
3. **The Vote**: Other players vote by pointing (or placing a token). Majority wins.
4. **Tiebreaker Options**:
   - The player who's losing in points wins (catch-up mechanic)
   - Rock-paper-scissors (quick, physical)
   - The player whose wizard school was founded first (historical priority — Archimedes beats everyone)
   - A coin flip with a dramatic "the universe decides" flavor
   - The player who makes the table laugh hardest (leans into the humor)

### Why It Might Work
- Creates "you had to be there" moments that people retell
- Encourages role-playing without requiring it
- Turns disputes into entertainment rather than frustration
- Fits the historical theme of priority disputes (Newton vs. Leibniz, Darwin vs. Wallace)

### Why It Might Not
- Slows the game down
- Favors extroverts — quiet players disadvantaged
- Hard to balance — voting can become political
- Some groups won't enjoy performative elements
- Could feel tacked-on if it doesn't integrate with other mechanics

### Compromise: Optional Variant Rule
Make it a **party variant** rather than the base game. The base game uses a clean priority rule (e.g., most knowledge tokens). The "Debate Mode" variant replaces this with the mock debate for groups who want it. Print it as a variant in the rulebook with its own section: **"The Academic Tribunal"**.

## Meme-Ready Marketing Moments

### Shareable Card Art

Cards should be designed so that **individual cards are shareable on social media** without context. The art + quote should be funny enough on its own. Think:
- MTG's most iconic card arts
- Exploding Kittens' card designs
- Cards Against Humanity's card text

### Social Media Hooks

| Format | Concept |
|--------|---------|
| **"Did you know?"** | Real science facts behind each card. "Did you know Marie Curie's notebooks are still radioactive? That's why her card glows." |
| **Card reveals** | Drip-feed card art before launch. Each one is a scientist in a funny situation. People share and tag friends. |
| **"Who would win?"** | Matchup posts. "Tesla (AC, wireless energy, pigeon army) vs. Edison (DC, elephant electrocution, patent trolling) — who wins?" |

| **Rivalry brackets** | March Madness-style tournament bracket of scientist rivalries. Community votes. |
| **Historical context posts** | "The real story behind [Card Name]" — short threads explaining the actual history. Educational + entertaining. |

### The "I REFUTE!" Moment

This is the game's **signature marketing moment**. The equivalent of:
- "UNO!" in Uno
- "JENGA!" in Jenga
- "I have a monopoly on..." in Monopoly
- Drawing an Exploding Kitten

When someone counters a play by yelling "I REFUTE!" — that's the moment people photograph, video, and share. The card should be designed to be visually dramatic when slammed on the table.

## Tone Guidelines for All Design Work

### Do
- Make scientists feel like characters, not textbook entries
- Use real history but present it with humor and warmth
- Design card art that makes people smile or laugh
- Create moments that generate stories ("Remember when you refuted my theorem?")
- Use academic language in absurd contexts ("Your sample size is ONE")
- Let players be dramatic — give them tools (phrases, big moments) to be theatrical
- Reference memes and pop culture where it fits naturally

### Don't
- Mock the science itself — the science should be accurate and respected
- Make the humor mean-spirited toward the scientists — we're celebrating them
- Be so referential that non-science people feel excluded
- Force humor — let it emerge from the collision of serious history and game context
- Make the game feel like it's trying too hard to be funny
- Sacrifice gameplay for a joke — humor supports mechanics, not the other way around

### The Tone Spectrum

```
Too Serious ←——————————————→ Too Silly
   PBS Documentary    |    Exploding Kittens
                      |
               [WE ARE HERE]
                      |
           Epic Rap Battles of History
              Drunk History
           Bill Nye the Science Guy
```

We're in the **"smart and funny"** zone. The humor comes from genuine knowledge presented with irreverence. You learn something AND you laugh.
