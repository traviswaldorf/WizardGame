## Major Rivalries

### 1. Newton vs. Leibniz — The Calculus Priority War

**Who:** Isaac Newton (English, 1643–1727) vs. Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (German, 1646–1716)

**What they fought about:** Who invented calculus first. Newton developed his "method of fluxions" in 1665–1666 but didn't publish until decades later. Leibniz independently developed his version in 1675–1676 and published in 1684. Newton claimed Leibniz stole his ideas after seeing unpublished manuscripts during a visit to London.

**Most dramatic moments:**
- Newton, as President of the Royal Society, appointed a committee to investigate the dispute in 1712 — then **secretly wrote the committee's report himself**, which unsurprisingly found in his favor. The report, "Commercium Epistolicum," was presented as an impartial investigation.
- Newton anonymously wrote a review of the Commercium Epistolicum praising it. He was judge, jury, prosecutor, and publicist.
- Newton reportedly said after Leibniz's death in 1716: **"I broke his heart."**
- Leibniz died in 1716 largely discredited in England, with his funeral attended by almost no one — his secretary was the only mourner.
- The feud damaged British mathematics for over a century. British mathematicians stubbornly used Newton's inferior dot notation while continental Europe advanced rapidly using Leibniz's far superior dy/dx notation.

**Who won:** Leibniz won the notation war — we all use his notation today. Modern consensus: both invented calculus independently. Newton probably got there first chronologically, but Leibniz published first and had the better system.

**Visual/memeable moments:** Newton secretly writing the "impartial" report judging himself the winner. Peak academic pettiness.

**Element mapping:** Newton → Earth (gravity, mass); Leibniz → not currently in game

### 2. Tesla vs. Edison — The War of Currents

**Who:** Nikola Tesla (Serbian-American, 1856–1943) vs. Thomas Edison (American, 1847–1931)

**What they fought about:** AC (alternating current, Tesla/Westinghouse) vs. DC (direct current, Edison). Also a personal grudge — Tesla worked for Edison early in his career, and Edison allegedly promised him $50,000 to improve his DC generators. When Tesla succeeded, Edison reportedly said **"Tesla, you don't understand our American humor"** and refused to pay. Tesla quit.

**Most dramatic moments:**
- Edison launched a propaganda campaign to prove AC was dangerous. He publicly **electrocuted dogs, cats, horses, and even a circus elephant named Topsy** using AC to demonstrate its lethality.
- Edison's team developed the **electric chair** using AC — specifically to associate Westinghouse/Tesla's current with death. Edison wanted electrocution to be called being "Westinghoused."
- The first electric chair execution (William Kemmler, 1890) was botched horribly — the first jolt didn't kill him. Westinghouse reportedly said: **"They would have done better using an axe."**
- Tesla would dramatically demonstrate AC's safety by letting current pass through his own body to light lamps at the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition.
- AC won decisively. It powered the 1893 World's Fair and the Niagara Falls power project (1896).

**Who won:** Tesla/AC won the technical battle completely. Edison won the fame/money battle — he died wealthy and celebrated; Tesla died alone and impoverished in a New York hotel room in 1943, obsessed with pigeons.

**Visual/memeable moments:** Edison electrocuting an elephant; Tesla calmly sitting in his laboratory reading while enormous electrical arcs crackle around him (the famous Colorado Springs photo); Tesla demonstrating AC by running current through himself.

**Element mapping:** Tesla → Magnetic (pupil A, "School of the Field"); Edison → not currently in game

### 3. Lavoisier vs. Priestley — The Oxygen Revolution

**Who:** Antoine Lavoisier (French, 1743–1794) vs. Joseph Priestley (English, 1733–1804)

**What they fought about:** The nature of combustion and the discovery of oxygen. Priestley isolated oxygen in 1774 (calling it "dephlogisticated air") and told Lavoisier about it during a dinner in Paris. Lavoisier then conducted his own experiments and correctly identified it as an element, naming it "oxygen" and using it to demolish the phlogiston theory that Priestley clung to until his death.

**Most dramatic moments:**
- Priestley literally told Lavoisier about his discovery over dinner, giving him the crucial clue. Lavoisier then used it to overthrow the entire theoretical framework Priestley believed in. Priestley felt robbed.
- Priestley **never accepted Lavoisier's oxygen theory**. He published "The Doctrine of Phlogiston Established" in 1800 — still defending phlogiston 26 years after isolating oxygen. He went to his grave defending a theory his own discovery disproved.
- Lavoisier was **executed by guillotine** during the French Revolution in 1794. The judge reportedly said: **"The Republic has no need of geniuses."**
- Lagrange said the next day: **"It took them only an instant to cut off that head, and a hundred years may not produce another like it."**
- Priestley's house and laboratory were burned by a mob in 1791 (the Priestley Riots). He fled to America.

**Who won:** Lavoisier won the science — he's called the "father of modern chemistry." But Priestley made the actual discovery. Both met dramatic, violent ends.

**Visual/memeable moments:** Lavoisier at the guillotine; Priestley's lab burning; the dinner party where Priestley handed Lavoisier the key to his own theory's destruction.

**Element mapping:** Lavoisier → Fire (head, "School of Combustion"); Priestley → Air (not in game)

### 4. Galileo vs. the Church

**Who:** Galileo Galilei (Italian, 1564–1642) vs. the Roman Catholic Church (especially Pope Urban VIII and the Inquisition)

**What they fought about:** Heliocentrism — whether the Earth orbits the Sun.

**Most dramatic moments:**
- Galileo wrote "Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems" (1632) as a supposedly balanced debate. The character defending the Church's position was named **"Simplicio"** (literally "simpleton"). Pope Urban VIII — who had been Galileo's friend — believed Galileo had put the Pope's own arguments into Simplicio's mouth. He was furious.
- At his trial before the Inquisition in 1633, Galileo was forced to kneel and recant. Legend has it he muttered: **"Eppur si muove"** ("And yet it moves"). Almost certainly apocryphal — saying that would have been suicidal — but it's one of the most famous quotes in science.
- Galileo was sentenced to house arrest for life. He went blind in 1638 but continued working.
- The Church didn't formally acknowledge Galileo was right until **1992** — 359 years later.

**Who won:** Galileo, but it took a while.

**Visual/memeable moments:** Galileo kneeling before the Inquisition then muttering "and yet it moves"; naming the Church's defender "Simplicio"; the 359-year lag.

**Element mapping:** Galileo → Cosmic or Light (astronomy, optics, telescope)

### 5. Darwin vs. Wallace — The Evolution Priority Question

**Who:** Charles Darwin (English, 1809–1882) vs. Alfred Russel Wallace (English/Welsh, 1823–1913)

**What they fought about:** Priority for the theory of evolution by natural selection. Darwin had been developing his theory for 20 years but hadn't published. In 1858, Wallace independently arrived at the same theory and **mailed his paper to Darwin**, asking him to forward it if worthy.

**Most dramatic moments:**
- Darwin received Wallace's letter and was horrified. He wrote: **"All my originality, whatever it may amount to, will be smashed."**
- Darwin's friends Lyell and Hooker arranged a joint presentation at the Linnean Society on July 1, 1858. Wallace wasn't even consulted — he was thousands of miles away and learned about it after the fact.
- The president of the Linnean Society later wrote that 1858 had been unremarkable, with **"no striking discoveries"** — completely missing evolution by natural selection.
- Wallace was remarkably gracious. He titled his own book "Darwinism" and always deferred to Darwin's priority.

**Who won:** Darwin won the fame — it's "Darwinism," not "Wallacism." Wallace is the "forgotten father of evolution."

**Visual/memeable moments:** Darwin's panic upon receiving Wallace's letter; the Linnean Society president declaring nothing notable happened that year.

**Element mapping:** Darwin → Plant (head, "School of Growth"); Wallace → Plant or Ghost (forgotten)

### 6. Bohr vs. Einstein — The Quantum Debates

**Who:** Niels Bohr (Danish, 1885–1962) vs. Albert Einstein (German-American, 1879–1955)

**What they fought about:** The interpretation of quantum mechanics. Einstein rejected indeterminacy. Bohr championed the Copenhagen interpretation. Their debates at the Solvay Conferences (1927, 1930) are among the most famous intellectual confrontations in history.

**Most dramatic moments:**
- Einstein: **"God does not play dice with the universe."** Bohr: **"Einstein, stop telling God what to do."**
- At the 1927 Solvay Conference, Einstein came to breakfast each morning with a new thought experiment to disprove the uncertainty principle. Bohr spent all day refuting it. Next morning, Einstein had another one. This went on for days.
- At the 1930 conference, Einstein presented his "photon box" thought experiment. Bohr was shaken and couldn't sleep. But overnight, Bohr realized Einstein's own general relativity saved the uncertainty principle. He used Einstein's own theory against him. Einstein conceded.
- Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen published the EPR paradox in 1935, arguing quantum mechanics was "incomplete." The physics community sided with Bohr.
- Despite fierce disagreement, deep mutual respect. Einstein called Bohr's atomic model "the highest form of musicality in the sphere of thought." Bohr kept a picture of Einstein in his office.
- The word on Bohr's blackboard at death in 1962 was a drawing of Einstein's photon box — still thinking about it.

**Who won:** Bohr won during their lifetimes. But Einstein may have the last laugh — quantum entanglement ("spooky action at a distance") is real and underpins quantum computing.

**Visual/memeable moments:** Einstein arriving at breakfast with thought experiments every morning; the photon box on Bohr's deathbed blackboard; "stop telling God what to do."

**Element mapping:** Einstein → Light (proposed Head, decision 007); Bohr → Light pupil or rival

### 7. Hooke vs. Newton — The Bitter Personal Feud

**Who:** Robert Hooke (English, 1635–1703) vs. Isaac Newton (English, 1643–1727)

**What they fought about:** Optics, gravity, and credit. Decades-long feud.

**Most dramatic moments:**
- Newton's **"If I have seen further, it is by standing on the shoulders of giants"** — often read as humble. But Hooke was notably short and physically deformed. Many historians believe Newton was making a **deliberate dig at Hooke's appearance**.
- When Hooke complained Newton stole his gravity ideas, Newton **systematically removed every reference to Hooke from the Principia**. Threatened to withhold Book III entirely rather than credit Hooke.
- After Hooke died in 1703, Newton became President of the Royal Society. The only known portrait of Hooke subsequently **disappeared**. Many historians believe Newton had it destroyed. To this day, no verified image of Hooke's face exists.
- Newton also oversaw the Royal Society's relocation, during which much of Hooke's legacy was "conveniently lost."

**Who won:** Newton, overwhelmingly. His campaign to erase Hooke from history was disturbingly effective.

**Visual/memeable moments:** Newton possibly destroying the only portrait of Hooke; "shoulders of giants" as an insult; purging the Principia.

**Element mapping:** Hooke → Earth or Crystal (elasticity, microscopy); Newton → Earth (head)

### 8. Pasteur vs. Pouchet — Spontaneous Generation

**Who:** Louis Pasteur (French, 1822–1895) vs. Félix-Archimède Pouchet (French, 1800–1872)

**What they fought about:** Whether life could arise spontaneously from non-living matter.

**Most dramatic moments:**
- Pasteur designed his famous **swan-neck flask** experiments — elegantly simple flasks that proved contamination came from airborne microorganisms.
- Pasteur was a brilliant showman. He challenged Pouchet to repeat experiments side by side. Pouchet agreed to a public contest judged by the French Academy — then **withdrew at the last minute**, claiming bias. Terrible optics.
- Pasteur dramatically took his flasks to mountaintops and into cellars to show microbe variation by location.
- Ironically, Pouchet wasn't entirely wrong — his hay infusions contained heat-resistant **endospores** that survived sterilization. His results were real, but his interpretation was wrong.
- Pasteur's private notebooks revealed he sometimes **cherry-picked results**.

**Who won:** Pasteur, decisively. "Father of germ theory."

**Visual/memeable moments:** The elegant swan-neck flasks; Pasteur on an alpine mountainside; Pouchet backing down.

**Element mapping:** Pasteur → Plant or Poison (biology, disease, fermentation)

### 9. Marie Curie — Conflicts and Drama

**Who:** Marie Curie (Polish-French, 1867–1934)

**Key conflicts:**
- **Curie vs. French Academy of Sciences (1911):** Despite two Nobel Prizes, she was **rejected for membership by two votes** — because she was a woman. The Academy didn't admit women until 1979.
- **Curie vs. tabloid press (1911):** Newspapers revealed her affair with physicist Paul Langevin. Called a **"foreign Jewish home-wrecker"** (she was neither Jewish nor the instigator). The Nobel committee asked her not to come accept her prize. She went to Stockholm anyway: **"The prize was awarded for the discovery of radium and polonium, and the scientific work should not be influenced by personal matters."**
- **Curie vs. those who diminished her:** After Pierre's death (run over by a horse-drawn carriage in 1906), Marie took over his Sorbonne professorship — the **first woman to hold one** — and proved her brilliance was entirely her own with the second Nobel.

**Dramatic details:**
- Carried test tubes of radioactive isotopes in her pockets, describing the "pretty blue-green glow."
- Her personal belongings — notebooks, cookbook, furniture — are still so radioactive they're **stored in lead-lined boxes**. You must sign a liability waiver and wear protective clothing to view them. They'll remain radioactive for another 1,500 years.

**Visual/memeable moments:** Her radioactive cookbook; signing a waiver to read her notebooks; refusing to stay home from the Nobel ceremony.

**Element mapping:** Curie → Radioactive (head, "School of the Atom")

### 10. Archimedes — The Original Scientific Drama

**Who:** Archimedes of Syracuse (Greek, c. 287–212 BC)

**Dramatic moments:**
- **"Eureka!"**: Allegedly realized volume displacement while bathing and **ran naked through the streets of Syracuse** shouting "Eureka!" Probably apocryphal but it's the foundational eureka moment in all of science.
- **Death by Roman soldier:** During the Siege of Syracuse (212 BC), Archimedes had designed war machines that held off the Roman army for two years. When Romans breached the walls, a soldier found him drawing geometric diagrams in sand. Archimedes said **"Do not disturb my circles"** and the soldier killed him. The Roman general had ordered him captured alive and was reportedly furious.
- **The heat ray debate:** Whether Archimedes used focused mirrors to set Roman ships on fire remains debated. MythBusters called it "busted"; MIT students succeeded under controlled conditions.

**Conflicts:** Archimedes vs. the Roman military is the original "nerd vs. jock" conflict on a civilizational scale. One mathematician held off the most powerful army in the world for two years.

**Element mapping:** Archimedes → Water (head, "School of Fluids")

### 11. Benjamin Franklin — Rivalries and Drama

**Who:** Benjamin Franklin (American, 1706–1790)

**Key conflicts and drama:**
- **Franklin vs. Abbé Nollet:** Nollet was France's leading electrical researcher and showman who once discharged a Leyden jar through a chain of **200 monks** to demonstrate electricity (they all jumped simultaneously). When Franklin's theories arrived in France, Nollet attacked them. Franklin mostly ignored him, which made it worse.
- **The kite experiment (1752):** Whether Franklin actually flew a kite in a thunderstorm is debated. What IS certain is that Georg Wilhelm Richmann tried to replicate a similar experiment and was **killed by ball lightning** — the first person to die doing electrical research.
- **Lightning rod politics:** Franklin advocated pointed rods; the British (backed by King George III, who hated Franklin) advocated blunt ones. The King ordered pointed rods removed. Sir John Pringle, Royal Society President, reportedly said: **"Sire, I cannot reverse the laws of nature."** He was forced to resign.
- **The turkey incident:** Franklin tried to kill a turkey using electrical discharge for a dinner party. He **shocked himself unconscious** instead. He wrote about it with characteristic humor, noting he'd intended to kill a turkey but nearly killed a goose (meaning himself).

**Visual/memeable moments:** Franklin shocking himself trying to electrocute a turkey; 200 monks jumping; a king trying to legislate lightning rod shapes.

**Element mapping:** Franklin → Electric (not currently in game as a wizard, but closely related to Electric school)

## Other Notable Rivalries

### Bone Wars: Cope vs. Marsh (1877–1892)

Rival paleontologists. The most absurdly personal feud in science.
- **Bribed each other's workers** to divert fossils
- **Dynamited fossil sites** rather than let the other access them
- Cope reconstructed an Elasmosaurus with the **head on the wrong end**. Marsh gleefully publicized this.
- Both spent their personal fortunes and died nearly broke
- Between them discovered over 130 dinosaur species

**Element mapping:** Both → Earth

### Eddington vs. Chandrasekhar (1930s)

Chandrasekhar, at age 19, calculated that massive stars would collapse into black holes. Eddington publicly humiliated him, calling it "stellar buffoonery" and saying **"there should be a law of nature to prevent a star from behaving in this absurd way."** Chandrasekhar got the Nobel in 1983. Eddington's bullying delayed the field by decades.

**Element mapping:** Chandrasekhar → Cosmic; Eddington → Light

### Semmelweis vs. the Medical Establishment (1840s–1860s)

Discovered that doctors washing hands reduced maternal deaths. The establishment rejected him because admitting doctors were killing patients was offensive. He was **committed to a mental asylum** in 1865 and beaten by guards, dying of septicemia — the very thing he'd tried to prevent. Now called **"The Semmelweis Reflex"** — rejecting evidence because it's inconvenient.

**Element mapping:** Semmelweis → Poison/Water

### Boltzmann vs. Mach and Ostwald (1890s–1906)

Boltzmann championed atoms and statistical mechanics. Mach and Ostwald denied atoms were real. The attacks contributed to Boltzmann's depression. He **committed suicide in 1906** — months before experiments proved atoms exist. His tombstone reads **S = k log W**.

**Element mapping:** Boltzmann → Ghost (head, "School of Entropy")

### Rosalind Franklin vs. Watson and Crick (1950s)

Franklin's X-ray crystallography image (Photo 51) was shown to Watson and Crick **without her consent**. Crucial to discovering DNA's double helix. She died of ovarian cancer (likely from radiation) in 1958. Watson's book described her dismissively. One of the most well-known cases of scientific credit theft.

**Element mapping:** Franklin (Rosalind) → Crystal

### Wegener vs. Everyone (Continental Drift, 1912–1960s)

Proposed continental drift in 1912. Geologists ridiculed him for decades, partly because he was a meteorologist. The American Association of Petroleum Geologists organized a symposium specifically to attack his theory. He died on a Greenland expedition in 1930. Plate tectonics wasn't accepted until the 1960s.

**Element mapping:** Wegener → Earth (pupil B, "School of Shifting Ground")

## Famous Dramatic / Funny Moments in Science

### Explosions and Accidents

- **Tycho Brahe** lost part of his nose in a sword duel (1566) **over who was the better mathematician**. Wore a prosthetic brass nose for life. Also died possibly from refusing to leave a banquet to urinate. Also kept a **pet moose that died from drinking too much beer at a party and falling down stairs**.
- **Hennig Brand** (1669) discovered phosphorus by **boiling 1,500 gallons of urine** looking for the philosopher's stone. The glowing substance was literally distilled from pee.
- **Humphry Davy** nearly killed himself multiple times inhaling experimental gases. Tested nitrous oxide (laughing gas) by huffing it in sealed chambers. Once inhaled **carbon monoxide** and was dragged outside unconscious. Wrote notes about each near-death experience.
- **Carl Scheele** had a habit of **taste-testing his chemical discoveries**, including hydrogen cyanide and mercury compounds. Died at 43.
- **The demon core** — a plutonium sphere at Los Alamos killed two physicists in criticality accidents. Louis Slotin was using a **screwdriver** to keep two beryllium hemispheres apart. His hand slipped, the room flashed blue, lethal dose. Called "tickling the dragon's tail."

### Eccentric Scientists

- **Nikola Tesla:** Fed pigeons obsessively. Fell in love with one specific white pigeon: **"I loved that pigeon as a man loves a woman, and she loved me."** Claimed to receive signals from Mars. Insisted on hotel room numbers divisible by three. Couldn't stand pearls.
- **Henry Cavendish:** So pathologically shy he communicated with servants by notes. Added a back staircase to avoid his housekeeper. **Ran away from women who spoke to him.** Despite this, one of the greatest experimental physicists ever.
- **Oliver Heaviside:** Replaced furniture with granite blocks. Painted fingernails pink. Contributed foundational work to electromagnetic theory.
- **Paul Dirac:** Extreme literal-mindedness. When told "it's raining outside," went to the window and said "yes." When a speaker said "I don't understand equation 2," Dirac replied: **"That was not a question, it was a statement."**
- **Richard Feynman:** Picked locks at Los Alamos during the Manhattan Project, leaving notes in classified safes. Played bongo drums. Once wrote to a woman arguing about his work: **"Ignore your husband's opinions on physics. He is a dope."**
- **J.B.S. Haldane:** When a decompression experiment perforated his eardrum, noted the **"gruesome whistling noise"** but said results were worth it. Wrote: **"The universe is not only queerer than we suppose, but queerer than we can suppose."**
- **Tycho Brahe:** See above — the nose, the moose, the beer, the banquet.

### Eureka Moments

- **Kekulé's dream of the snake** (1865): Claimed he discovered benzene's ring structure after dreaming of a snake eating its own tail (ouroboros). Possibly a convenient story.
- **Archimedes' bathtub** — the original eureka. See above.
- **Newton's apple** — probably didn't hit him on the head, but he did observe falling apples. The story comes from Newton himself in old age.
