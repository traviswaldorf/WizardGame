# Decision: Spell Terminology — Scientific Framing for Game Actions

> **ID**: 003
> **Status**: Exploring
> **Date Opened**: 2026-03-21
> **Date Decided**: —
> **Related Docs**: [design-vision.md](../philosophy/design-vision.md), [discovery-experience.md](../philosophy/discovery-experience.md), [glossary.md](../framework/glossary.md), [spell-archetypes.md](../philosophy/spell-archetypes.md)

## Context

Throughout the design docs, player actions are called "spells." This is standard fantasy game language but conflicts with the game's core identity: **science IS magic, discovery IS the spell.** If the game is about reimagining scientists as wizards and grounding everything in real physics/chemistry/biology, the act of playing a card should feel like conducting science, not waving a wand.

The right terminology should:
- Reinforce the "discovery as magic" principle
- Feel natural in gameplay speech ("I play a ___")
- Work as a card type label
- Scale across power levels (basic actions vs. Super Powers)
- Not be so academic that it alienates casual players

## Options

### Option A: Spells (Current)

**Description**: Keep the current terminology. Cards are spells, you cast spells.

**Pros**:
- Universally understood — zero learning curve
- Already established across all type docs
- "Casting a spell" has satisfying dramatic weight

**Cons**:
- Generic fantasy — could be any card game
- No connection to the scientific discovery theme
- Misses a chance to reinforce the game's unique identity at every card play

**Interactions**: Neutral with all mechanics. Doesn't reinforce or undermine the theme.

---

### Option B: Experiments

**Description**: Cards are experiments. You "run" or "conduct" an experiment. The outcome is the effect.

**Pros**:
- Extremely accessible — everyone knows what an experiment is
- Natural gameplay language: "I run an experiment," "my experiment failed"
- Captures the uncertainty/excitement of science — you try something and see what happens
- Could tie to mechanics: experiments with variable outcomes, coin flips, conditional effects
- Works great as a card label

**Cons**:
- Implies uncertainty — but many game actions should feel reliable, not risky
- Doesn't distinguish between power levels (a basic action and a devastating attack are both "experiments")
- Slightly clinical

**Interactions**: Strong synergy with Fire/Radioactive randomness mechanics (coin flips = experimental uncertainty). Could conflict with control-oriented types (Crystal, Ice) where precision, not experimentation, is the identity.

**Inspiration**: Science fair culture, lab notebooks, the experimental method.

---

### Option C: Hypotheses

**Description**: Cards are hypotheses. Playing a card means proposing and testing a hypothesis. The effect resolving means it was proven correct.

**Pros**:
- Deep scientific method tie-in — the scientific method starts with a hypothesis
- "I test my hypothesis" has dramatic weight — will it work?
- A countered hypothesis = a refuted hypothesis (thematically perfect)
- Fits the "trying to discover" angle — you don't know if it'll work until you try

**Cons**:
- Awkward plural (hypotheses) and long word — clunky on cards and in speech
- "I cast a hypothesis" doesn't roll off the tongue
- Too academic for casual players
- Implies everything is unproven, which undermines powerful late-game actions

**Interactions**: Strong synergy with the counter mechanic — countering = refuting. Could pair with a "proven" mechanic where hypotheses that resolve successfully become stronger on replay.

---

### Option D: Theories

**Description**: Cards are theories. Playing a card means applying a theory. "I apply a theory," "deploy a theory."

**Pros**:
- In science, a theory is well-supported and powerful (Theory of Relativity, Germ Theory)
- Carries weight and authority — "deploying a theory" feels impactful
- Clean word, works well on cards
- "My theory was disproven" = countered (thematic)

**Cons**:
- In common usage, "theory" implies speculation ("it's just a theory") — the opposite of its scientific meaning
- Somewhat passive — theories describe, they don't act
- Doesn't capture the excitement of discovery, more the confidence of established knowledge

**Interactions**: Could create interesting tension with the common misunderstanding of "theory" — a teaching moment baked into the game.

---

### Option E: Theses

**Description**: Cards are theses (singular: thesis). Playing a card means presenting/defending a thesis.

**Pros**:
- Academic and scholarly — fits the wizard school lore
- "I present my thesis" / "I defend my thesis" has dramatic flair
- A thesis is something you argue for and defend — maps to attack/defense naturally
- "Thesis defense" is a real thing — countering becomes "challenging the thesis"

**Cons**:
- Awkward plural (theses)
- Very academic — may alienate casual players
- Implies a formal, slow process — doesn't fit fast aggressive play

**Interactions**: Strong fit with the wizard school concept. Less natural for aggressive types like Fire.

---

### Option F: Tiered Scientific Method

**Description**: Instead of one term for all cards, use a **progression of scientific terms** that maps to card power level or category. The terminology mirrors how real science progresses from observation to established law.

| Card Tier / Role | Term | Scientific Meaning | Gameplay Feel |
|------------------|------|--------------------|---------------|
| Basic / cheap actions | **Hypothesis** | Untested idea | Quick, speculative, might fizzle |
| Standard actions | **Experiment** | Testing in action | The core gameplay — trying things |
| Powerful / proven actions | **Theory** | Well-supported knowledge | Reliable, impactful, hard to counter |
| Super Powers | **Law** | Fundamental truth | Undeniable, game-changing (Newton's Laws, Thermodynamics) |
| Permanent effects | **Theorem** | Proven result | Established, persists on the board |
| Counter / reactive | **Refutation** | Disproving a claim | Denying an opponent's science |

**Pros**:
- Mirrors real scientific progression — reinforces the theme at every level
- Creates intuitive power scaling: hypothesis < experiment < theory < law
- "I invoke the Law of Thermodynamics" is an incredible gameplay moment
- Each term teaches real scientific concepts through play
- Super Powers as "Laws" feels earned and authoritative
- Refutation as the counter mechanic is thematically perfect

**Cons**:
- Multiple terms to learn — higher complexity than a single word
- Players need to understand the hierarchy
- May over-systematize what should feel fluid
- Card layout needs to communicate tier clearly

**Interactions**: Strong synergy with the progression principle — games mirror scientific progress. Laws as Super Powers connects to real physics (Laws of Motion, Laws of Thermodynamics). Refutation as countering ties beautifully to Air/Ice/Crystal control identities.

**Inspiration**: The actual scientific method hierarchy. Academic progression (hypothesis → thesis → theory).

---

### Option G: Formulae

**Description**: Cards are formulae (singular: formula). Playing a card means applying a formula.

**Pros**:
- Strong alchemical/scientific feel — formulae bridge old magic and modern science
- "I apply a formula" is clean and active
- Works well on cards as a label
- Implies precision and reproducibility — science, not superstition
- E = mc² is a formula — connects to real science

**Cons**:
- Formulae feels mathematical, not experimental — misses the discovery excitement
- Doesn't scale well across power levels
- "My formula was countered" is less dramatic than other options

**Interactions**: Strong fit with Crystal (precision, structure). Less natural for chaotic types like Fire or Radioactive.

---

### Option H: Discoveries

**Description**: Cards are discoveries. Playing a card means making a discovery.

**Pros**:
- Directly embodies the "discovery as magic" principle
- "I make a discovery" has wonder and excitement
- A "discovery" feels like something new being revealed — matches the game's explorer spirit
- Clean, accessible word

**Cons**:
- Implies finding something new every time — but you replay cards, so they're not "new" after the first use
- Passive connotation — discoveries happen to you, you don't aim them at opponents
- "I discovery your tower" doesn't work — the verb form is awkward for targeted effects

**Interactions**: Strong thematic fit but weak mechanical fit for combat actions.

## Brainstorm: Gameplay Language Test

How does each term sound in common gameplay moments?

| Moment | Spells | Experiments | Hypotheses | Theories | Formulae | Discoveries |
|--------|--------|-------------|------------|----------|----------|-------------|
| Playing a card | "I cast a spell" | "I run an experiment" | "I test a hypothesis" | "I apply a theory" | "I apply a formula" | "I make a discovery" |
| Countering | "I counter your spell" | "I sabotage your experiment" | "I refute your hypothesis" | "I disprove your theory" | "I break your formula" | "I debunk your discovery" |
| Card in hand | "I have 3 spells" | "I have 3 experiments" | "I have 3 hypotheses" | "I have 3 theories" | "I have 3 formulae" | "I have 3 discoveries" |
| Powerful play | "Epic spell!" | "Breakthrough experiment!" | "Proven hypothesis!" | "Grand theory!" | "Master formula!" | "Major discovery!" |
| Discard pile | "Spent spells" | "Failed experiments" | "Disproven hypotheses" | "Outdated theories" | "Broken formulae" | "Old discoveries" |

## Emerging Leanings

- **Refutation as the counter mechanic is strongly favored.** Regardless of which option wins for the general card term, "Refutation" for counters is great lore — it captures the scientific discourse of challenging and disproving claims.
- **Option F (Tiered) is the most promising direction**, particularly because of the progression mechanic it enables (see below).

## Progression Mechanic: Scientific Advancement

The tiered terminology naturally maps to a **progression/leveling mechanic** where players must advance through the scientific method to unlock higher-tier actions. This reinforces the game's third design principle (Progression and Advancement) at a mechanical level, not just thematic.

### Core Idea

Players start each game at the **Hypothesis** level — they can only play basic, speculative actions. Through investment (spending resources, building pillars/towers, meeting conditions), they advance through the tiers, unlocking more powerful action categories.

| Tier | Term | Access | Feel |
|------|------|--------|------|
| 1 | **Hypothesis** | Available from start | Speculative, cheap, unreliable |
| 2 | **Experiment** | Unlocked through early investment | Core gameplay — testing and iterating |
| 3 | **Theory** | Unlocked through sustained progress | Proven, reliable, powerful |
| 4 | **Law** | Unlocked as ultimate achievement | Undeniable, game-changing (Super Powers) |

### Why This Works

- **Games mirror scientific progress** — you literally walk the path from speculation to fundamental truth
- **Engine-building through compounding discovery** — each tier builds on the last, not just in power but in the metaphor of knowledge deepening
- **Creates meaningful choice** — do you rush to Theories, or do you stay at Experiments and run many cheap actions?
- **Lore payoff** — a player who reaches "Law" status has truly mastered their element. Invoking the "Law of Thermodynamics" or "Newton's Second Law" feels earned because you progressed to get there
- **Natural power gating** — prevents powerful cards from being played too early without feeling like an arbitrary restriction

### Open Questions

- [ ] What investment unlocks each tier? Resource spending? Number of pillars/towers? Turns elapsed? Cards played?
- [ ] Is progression per-type (advance in Fire separately from Water) or global (advance your wizard overall)?
- [ ] Can you regress? If a pillar/tower is destroyed, do you lose access to a tier?
- [ ] How does this interact with multi-type cards? Do you need Theory-level in both types?
- [ ] Does each type have its own named Laws, or are Laws generic? (Named Laws feel much better)
- [ ] Where do Theorems (permanent effects) and Refutations (counters) sit in the tier hierarchy, or are they parallel categories available at any tier?

## Recommendation

Leaning strongly toward **Option F (Tiered Scientific Method)** — not just as terminology but as a **core progression mechanic**. The tiered approach turns the card system into both a mirror of real science and an engine-building progression system. **Refutation** is favored for the counter mechanic regardless of the final choice.

**Option B (Experiments)** remains the clean fallback if tiering proves too complex.
